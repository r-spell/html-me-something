Adventures in Stenciling

My History with Stenciling

I have been stenciling since I was in high school (so about 15 years). Most of that time I was doing stencils, by hand, with contact paper and x-acto knives. My earliest works were generally just black and white and were based on hand-drawn images or images altered with GIMP. Over the years I have done more with multicolor prints with stencils. I use GIMP and Inkscape to help me design stencils which make up different color layers to create a multicolor print. In the past year, I have also started using a laser cutter to make stencils. I belong to Arch Reactor, a community space for makers, which has a laser cutter members can use.

Problem Solving & Stenciling

I like stenciling because it brings together creative problem solving techniques to make something artistic. While simple "silhouette" images, can be quite easy to cut out, it takes some creativity to make more complex images with stencils. For example: it is easy to cut a heart, a star, or a silhouette of Elvis' head.[1]

The problem-solving element comes in as we look at images, which have "islands"" in them. At one point I did a Research Experience for Undergraduates[2] and we had a running thing about K4, a connected graph on 4 vertices. We decided to make t-shirts and I realized I could do them cheaply if I used stencils.

For the "Easy" stencils, we could just cut around the outside of the shapes. However, if we cut around the edge here, some of the paper gets cut away and lost, we lose the "islands."

To solve this problem, I made two stencils. One which gives the edges (lines) and one which gives the vertices (dots) of the K4. You may note the lines do not quite meet at the corners. This is on purpose. We call these little gaps, "bridges." If these lines were extended to the corners we would lose the "islands."
